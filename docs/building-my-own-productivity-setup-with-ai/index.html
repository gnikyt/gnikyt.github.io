Building my own productivity setup with AI /

Jun 01, 2026  —  7.8KB
#thoughts

For a long time, productivity tools have felt a little difficult to me. The tools either did too much, too little, or something in-between… it wasn’t “tailored” to me.

You start with a simple need: remember what needs doing, see what is on the calendar, keep track of notes, maybe find something you wrote down last week. Then somehow the solution becomes three subscriptions, a new dashboard, another app to maintain, another database, another login, another sync process, another place where your own personal information now lives.

I do not want more systems to manage the system.

What I wanted was simpler: use the tools I already use, keep the data where it already lives, and have ChatGPT act as the ingestion layer. So that is what I started building.

Why ChatGPT? Eh… I always used it since early launch. I have a great productivity flow with it for work and personal ops, it works well for me, and thats what matters!

Source of truth

For personal and work, I use Apple Calendar, Reminders, Mail, and Notes. They are boring in the best way. They are already on my phone, my laptop, etc. They already sync well. They already fit into my day without needing me to become a part-time operations manager for my own life.

So instead of replacing them, I built around them.

The idea is simple:

  • Calendar stays in Apple Calendar.
  • Tasks stay in Apple Reminders.
  • Notes stay in Apple Notes.
  • Mailing stays in Apple Mail.
  • ChatGPT becomes the front-end for asking questions and taking action.

That means I can ask things like:

What is my day like?

And behind the scenes, it can pull today’s calendar, overdue reminders, today’s reminders, and whatever other context matters. Then it can summarize it like a useful assistant instead of making me open four apps before coffee. Pairing this with existing integrations into services like JIRA, Slack, Confluence, and so on… it gives me a good picture of things.

On top of this, ChatGPT also handles recording my meetings, transcribing them and placing the summarized notes into the applicable folders in my Apple Notes.

Local-first, not SaaS-first

I could have used some polished third-party productivity service for this. There are plenty of them. Some are probably great.

But I did not want to pay monthly for a tool whose main job is to sit between me and data I already own.

I also did not want to sync my Apple Notes into a separate database just so I could search them with AI. That felt like adding risk and complexity for something that should be boring.

So I kept it local-first.

I built a local Apple/Home productivity MCP setup. It talks to Apple Calendar, Reminders, Notes, etc directly. For Notes, I added an answer_from_notes action that searches live, retrieves only matching note bodies for that request, strips them down to plain text, and answers from those results only.

No background indexing. No embeddings. No separate notes database. No “sync all your private notes into this clever new thing.”

Just live queries against the source of truth.

That distinction matters to me. AI tooling can get weirdly hungry if you let it. Suddenly everything wants to index everything, store everything, summarize everything, and “remember” everything forever. I do not need that for day-to-day ops. I need the right information at the right moment, then I need it to get out of the way.

The runner

The other piece is what I call Home Runner. It is the local orchestrator that keeps the required pieces alive.

This is not glamorous software. It is glue. But glue is underrated.

It is an Apple app which allows me to configure custom services to run… naming, envs, paths, notes, logs, etc. So I can start, stop, restart, and monitor various services (like the custom MCPs).

All these services and MCPs run through a custom domain and tunnel setup to be accessible through ChatGPT and only via my devices.

What this gives me

The most useful interaction is the daily one.

I can ask:

What is my day like?

And the assistant can pull from Calendar and Reminders, then give me a grounded summary of meetings, deadlines, overdue tasks, and open space.

I can ask:

Show my reminders.

Or:

Show recent notes.

Or:

What have I discussed with this person lately?

That last one is where Apple Notes becomes much more useful. I can keep using notes the way I naturally use them, then ask a question later and have the assistant search them live. It is not trying to become a second brain product. It is just helping me retrieve context from the first brain dump.

Again, nothing magical. Just a conversational layer over things I already track.

Combining this with the other MCP setups like Jira, Conflurence, Slack, etc. gives me a nice picture.

The part AI is actually good at

This is where AI has felt genuinely useful to me.

Not as a magical replacement for thinking. Not as a productivity personality cult. Not as another subscription box with charts.

AI is useful here because it can take several small pieces of context and turn them into a reasonable answer.

Calendar events by themselves are not the day. Reminders by themselves are not the day. Notes by themselves are not the day.

But together, in the right moment, they become context.

That is the actual value.

Instead of asking me to organize my life around a new tool, ChatGPT can adapt to the tools already in my life.

You can build this too

The nice thing is that none of this requires a big company-scale system.

Start small.

Pick the source of truth you already trust. Maybe that is Apple Notes. Maybe it is Google Calendar. Maybe it is a folder of Markdown files. Maybe it is a local SQLite database. The important part is to avoid inventing a new storage layer unless you truly need one.

Then expose a few useful actions:

  • Get today’s calendar.
  • Get due and overdue reminders.
  • Search notes.
  • Retrieve one note.
  • Append to a note.
  • Ask a grounded question from matching notes.

That is already enough to make a daily assistant feel useful.

From there, add only the parts that remove friction. If you take meeting notes, add a workflow that turns a transcript into summary and action items, then saves it back to Notes. If tunnels break often, add health checks.

I have taken the same approach with other little tools too, like a home budget system and a custom CLI for remembering commands I do not want to keep re-looking-up. They are not meant to become platforms. They are just small, useful things built around the way I already work.

But resist the urge to turn it into a giant platform.

That is how productivity systems become their own chores.

I vibe-coded all the Apple apps and MCP implementations in a couple hours and they worked fantastically.

The shape of it

The setup I want is not a dashboard.

It is closer to a command center, but even that sounds too dramatic. Really, it is a quiet bridge between the tools I already use and the questions I naturally ask.

I do not want to open Calendar, Reminders, and Notes just to understand my morning.

I want to ask:

What should I pay attention to today?

And get a useful answer.

That is the whole thing.

AI did not replace my productivity tools. It made them easier to talk to. And by keeping the source of truth local and boring, I get the benefit without handing my whole personal workflow to another third-party service.

That feels like the right shape to me: Apple apps for capture, local services for access, ChatGPT for conversation, and as little extra machinery as possible. You don’t need to reinvent the wheel, add five subscriptions to your wallet, or go crazy migrating data… utilize what works for you already.

End of the day, I am happy… it is useful, boring, and mine.
