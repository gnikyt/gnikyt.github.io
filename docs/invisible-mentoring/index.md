I've been building software for about 25 years now. Somewhere in that time, without really planning for it, I became a person people came to when they were stuck... not just on a bug, but on a decision, a way of thinking through something, a direction they couldn't quite see yet, another set of eyes n' ears for them.

I've led teams, helped run an agency, consulted across a lot of different industries, and worked alongside developers at every level. And the thing I've learned about mentoring is that the most effective version of it is mostly invisible while it's happening.

## What most people picture

When people say mentoring, they usually mean something formal. A senior person and a junior person, scheduled time, knowledge transferred downward. The senior explains how things work, the junior listens and takes notes.

That version is real and it has its place. Onboarding, specific skills, explicit gaps... sometimes the right move is just to explain something directly and clearly.

But a lot of that kind of mentoring fades fast. The person understood in the moment, and two weeks later they're back to the same pattern, not because they weren't paying attention, but because *being told something* and *working something out yourself* are different experiences, and only one of them actually changes how you think. It's like when you were in school and the teacher would have you write down everything on the chalkboard, to help mentally record it because you end up doing it.

## Code review that isn't a code review

The temptation when you spot something going sideways is to just say it. Pull them aside, explain what you're seeing, tell them what you'd do instead. Clean, direct, done. And sometimes that's exactly the right call.
 
But more often, I'll ask them to walk me through it, or comment on the issue in a way that helps them think to walk themselves through it. In a code review, over a thread, in a 1:1 huddle... wherever it naturally fits. And then I ask genuine questions. Not leading questions designed to expose the flaw, actual ones: *"how does this hold up when the dataset gets larger?"* or *"how does this work with multi-instance?* or *"what would need to change if we had X situation happen here?"*

The goal is to get us both looking at the same thing from the same angle. When you ask someone to explain their own work out loud, they often hear the problem before you say a word... just like our ol' faithful Rubber Duck Debugging. And when they do, something different happens than if you'd pointed it out yourself... they own the insight, it clicks internally, it came **from them**. That changes what they do with it.

If they don't land on it themselves, the questions have still moved things into territory where bringing it up feels collaborative rather than corrective. *"I've been noticing something similar in a few other places, I'm trying to figure out the right pattern, what do you think?"* is a very different conversation than *"here's what you should be doing instead."*

One ends with someone who has a new approach. The other ends with someone who has a new approach and learned experience that came from themselves, by you nudging them in the right direction.

## Designing the situation

That code review example is about a specific moment. The longer game is something more deliberate, like noticing where someone has a ceiling they don't know they have, and designing situations that let them grow into rather than telling them it's there.

This looks like giving someone a problem that's slightly above their current level. Not so far that they flounder, but far enough that it requires them to stretch, like a seedling, to **grow**. And then staying nearby, but without *hovering*, just available in arm's reach if they hit a wall, quiet enough that they get the chance to push through it first.

There's a real skill in calibrating that distance. Too close and you become a crutch they learn on for every single problem they feel is out of their wheel house, or stuck on... they start waiting for you before they even try, sort of like using AI as a crutch. Too far and they're stuck longer than they needed to be, and that's not a growth experience, it's just a bad afternoon. The right distance is where they come to you *after* they've tried a path or two, not before.

What you're doing in those moments isn't really teaching. You're creating the conditions for someone to figure it out themselves, gain independence, critical thinking skills, all while having you as a resource rather than *the* source.

## Patience you won't always have

To be fair, this approach is slower, 100%. Explaining something directly is faster and easier than engineering a situation where someone works it out themselves. If you're under pressure, short-staffed, or up against a deadline that doesn't care about anyone's personal development, the indirect approach is a luxury you might not have.

There are also people it just doesn't land with. Some people want direct feedback. Some read questions as indirectness or a kind of game. Some have worked in environments where questions meant criticism, and that association runs deep. Misreading which kind of person you're dealing with and defaulting to the indirect approach anyway can come across as evasive.

Direct and honest feedback delivered well isn't a failure of mentoring... it's often the most respectful thing you can do. The skill isn't in avoiding it. It's in knowing when each approach actually fits.

## What you're really building

The reason this kind of mentoring is underrated is that it doesn't look like much while it's happening. There's no visible moment of transfer. It's quieter than that.

But what you're building, over time, is someone who thinks differently. And not because you told them to think differently, but because they've practiced arriving at good answers through their own reasoning, with you asking the right questions at the right moments. That compounds. The next time they face something similar, the internal conversation you helped them have is one they can have themselves.

The best outcome,and I've seen this play out a handful of times over the years, which is enough: is that the person doesn't think of you as their mentor at all. They think of you as a colleague who helped them figure something out. The mentoring was invisible because it felt like collaboration.

That's the version that sticks.
